SLIDE 4: MEDS EXTRACTION - 7 CRITICAL STAGES
=============================================
[Duration: 2 minutes]

"Now let's dive into the technical pipeline, starting with MEDS extraction - the process of converting raw hospital CSV files into a standardized clinical timeline format.

[Follow the flow diagram from top to bottom]

Stage 1: Shard Events - We take massive CSV files and split them by patient groups. This enables parallel processing across multiple CPU cores, dramatically speeding up computation.

Stage 2: Split Subjects - Here we divide our 395 patients into training and testing groups. We used a 90/10 split, giving us 356 patients for training the model and 39 for testing its predictions.

Stage 3: Convert to Sharded Events - This is where the magic happens. We transform flat database tables into chronological event sequences. For example, instead of seeing 'Patient ID, diagnosis code, date' in separate columns, we create a timeline: 'On May 20th at 2:30pm, Patient 10000032 was diagnosed with J96.00.'

Stage 4: Merge to MEDS Cohort - Individual patient timelines get consolidated into unified training and testing datasets. Think of it as taking 356 separate patient stories and binding them into one comprehensive medical textbook.

Stage 5: Extract Code Metadata - We build a dictionary of every clinical code that appears in our data, along with how frequently each code occurs. This helps us filter out rare codes that might just be data entry errors or extremely uncommon conditions.

Stage 6: Finalize MEDS Metadata - We create comprehensive statistics about our dataset: how many events per patient on average, what's the distribution of diagnoses, temporal patterns, etc.

Stage 7: Finalize MEDS Data - The final validation step ensures everything is properly formatted and ready for the next stage.

[Point to output]
After all this, we have 356 training patients in a 115KB file and 39 test patients in a 23KB file. Notice how small these are - that's the power of efficient data formatting.

[Emphasize bottom banner]
The beauty of MEDS format is interoperability. A model trained on MIMIC-IV data can potentially work with Stanford Health data or UF Health data, as long as everyone uses MEDS format. This is crucial for advancing healthcare AI."

SLIDE 5: COMPUTATIONAL WORKFLOW
================================

"One of the unique aspects of this project was orchestrating work across three different computing environments. Let me walk you through this journey, because it illustrates real-world constraints in healthcare AI research.

[Start at top - HyperGator]
We begin at HyperGator, the University of Florida's supercomputing cluster. This is where the full MIMIC-IV dataset lives - 45 gigabytes of protected patient data. For security and access reasons, this data can't just be downloaded to any personal computer. 

On HyperGator, we performed two critical operations: First, we extracted our 395-patient sample from the full dataset. Second, we ran all 7 MEDS extraction stages. This took about 15 minutes with 4 parallel CPU cores.

[Follow arrow down]
Then we used SCP - Secure Copy Protocol - to download just the processed MEDS files. These were only 138 kilobytes, small enough to transfer easily.

[Point to Local Windows]
The data arrives at my local Windows laptop, which has an NVIDIA GTX 1650 GPU with 4GB of video memory. This GPU is crucial for training neural networks efficiently. Initially, we planned to do everything here.

[Point to arrow to WSL]
But we hit a roadblock. Tokenization creates temporary files with timestamps in their names, like 'train:2180-07-23T06:38:00'. Windows filesystems don't allow colons in filenames - it's a fundamental operating system limitation dating back to MS-DOS.

So we pivoted to WSL - Windows Subsystem for Linux. This gives us a Linux environment running inside Windows, which handles colons in filenames perfectly. We performed all 17 tokenization stages here over about 20 minutes.

[Point to final arrow back to Windows]
Once tokenization completed, we transferred the processed files back to Windows for GPU training. The model trained in just 6 minutes on the GTX 1650, and inference on 25 test cases took 24 seconds.

[Emphasize 'Why This Architecture']
This multi-environment approach highlights real-world constraints: protected data stays on secure clusters, GPU acceleration happens on local hardware, and we adapt to filesystem limitations dynamically. It's messy, but it's realistic, and it taught us a lot about deployment challenges we'll face in production."

SLIDE 6: TOKENIZATION PIPELINE - 17 STAGES
===========================================
[Duration: 2 minutes]

"Tokenization is where clinical language becomes mathematical language. We go from text like 'acute respiratory failure' to numbers like [71, 69, 4, 28]. Let me break down the 17 stages by their functional purpose.

[Point to Filtering section]
Filtering stages remove noise. We started with 892 unique clinical codes but found many appeared only once or twice - likely data entry errors or extremely rare conditions. By keeping only codes that appear at least 5 times, we reduced to 245 reliable codes. This improves model learning by focusing on common, clinically significant patterns.

[Point to Preprocessing section]
Preprocessing is the heavy lifting. We transform ICD diagnosis codes into hierarchical formats. For example, the code J96.00 becomes two tokens: '3-6//J96' representing the broader category, and 'SFX//J96.00' for the specific code. This helps the model learn at multiple levels of granularity - like understanding both 'respiratory disease' and 'acute respiratory failure specifically.'

We also extract patient static data - things like gender and birth year that don't change over time.

[Point to Quantization section]
Quantization converts continuous numbers into categories. If we had lab results, a glucose level of 145 might become 'Q4' meaning 'fourth quintile' or 'high glucose.' Our sample didn't have lab data, so these stages passed through unchanged, but they're critical for full datasets.

[Point to Temporal Modeling section]
Here's where it gets interesting. Temporal modeling injects time interval tokens between events. If a patient is admitted on Monday and goes to ICU on Wednesday, we insert a '=2d' token meaning 'two days later.' This teaches the model temporal patterns - like learning that ICU admission typically happens 2-3 days after hospital admission for certain conditions.

This step added 5,777 interval tokens to our 11,652 event tokens, increasing to 17,429 total tokens.

[Point to Vocabulary Building]
Finally, we count every code across train and test data, then assign each unique code an integer ID from 0 to 71. This creates our vocabulary - the model's dictionary.

[Point to result]
The output is patient timelines converted to sequences of integers, ready for a transformer model to process."

SLIDE 7: TECHNICAL CHALLENGES & SOLUTIONS
==========================================
[Duration: 2.5 minutes]

"No machine learning project goes smoothly, and ours was no exception. We encountered over 20 technical challenges. Let me highlight the five most significant and how we solved them.

[Point to Challenge 1]
Configuration Mismatch: The MEDS extraction code was written for the full MIMIC-IV dataset with all 11 tables. When it looked for 'transfers.csv' and 'labevents.csv' and didn't find them, it crashed immediately. 

Our solution was to create a simplified configuration file - 'event_configs-sample.yaml' - that listed only the 5 tables we actually had. We then modified the extraction script to use this simplified config. This taught us an important lesson: configuration files need to be flexible for different dataset scales.

[Point to Challenge 2]
Windows Filesystem Limitations: As I mentioned earlier, colons in filenames are forbidden on Windows. When tokenization tried to create 'train:2180-07-23T06:38:00.parquet', Windows threw an error.

Rather than trying to rewrite the filename generation code, we simply moved to WSL Ubuntu. This gave us Linux compatibility while staying within the Windows environment. Sometimes the best solution is changing environments rather than changing code.

[Point to Challenge 3]
Missing Clinical Features: This was the most pervasive issue. The preprocessing code was written assuming full MIMIC-IV data with lab results, medication records, detailed demographic information. Our sample had none of that.

We encountered 20+ errors like 'KeyError: text_value column not found.' Each time, we added a defensive check: 'If this column exists, process it; otherwise, skip it.' We modified 15+ functions with these checks. This defensive programming pattern is now embedded throughout our codebase and makes it robust to different dataset configurations.

[Point to Challenge 4]
Vocabulary Gaps: Here's a subtle but critical bug. The vocabulary was built from tokenized events. But patient demographics like 'GENDER//M' were collected in a separate static data stage that ran after vocabulary creation. When training tried to use token ID for 'GENDER//M', it wasn't in the vocabulary - crash.

We wrote a script to scan the static data file, find all codes that weren't in the vocabulary, and add them. This expanded our vocabulary from 69 to 72 tokens. It's a small fix but critical for training success.

[Point to Challenge 5]
Device Mismatch in Training: During training, the model lived on the GPU, but when validation ran, the data was still on CPU. PyTorch threw an error: 'Expected all tensors on same device.'

We modified the metrics calculation code to explicitly move validation data to GPU before running inference. This seems obvious in hindsight, but it's easy to overlook when you're deep in development.

[Emphasize key takeaway]
The key takeaway is systematic debugging. Every error taught us something about the pipeline's assumptions. By documenting and fixing each issue, we built a robust system that can handle diverse dataset configurations."

SLIDE 8: POST-TOKENIZATION DATA PREPARATION
============================================
[Duration: 2 minutes]

"After tokenization completes, we're not quite ready for training yet. There's a crucial data preparation phase that converts human-readable parquet files into machine-optimized tensor files. Let me walk through the 5-step process.

[Point to Step 1]
We load the final tokenized parquet files - 17,429 events from 356 training patients and 1,827 events from 39 test patients. These are still in text format with codes like 'ICD10CM//J96.00' and 'GENDER//M'.

[Point to Step 2]
We build the vocabulary by extracting every unique code from both train and test data. We add four special tokens that every language model needs: PAD for padding short sequences, UNK for unknown codes, START and END for sequence boundaries. Then we assign each code an integer ID from 0 to 71.

[Point to Step 3]
This is where we discovered those missing demographic codes I mentioned earlier. We wrote a script to scan the static data pickle file, found GENDER//M, GENDER//F, and MEDS_BIRTH were missing, and added them to the vocabulary. This brought us from 69 to 72 tokens.

[Point to Step 4]
Static data was collected separately for train and test patients. We merged these into one unified file covering all 395 patients. This ensures the model can look up any patient's demographics during training or inference.

[Point to Step 5]
Finally, we convert everything to safetensors format. Patient timelines become sequences of token IDs. For example, the sequence 'MEDS_BIRTH, GENDER//M, HOSPITAL_ADMISSION, ICD10CM//3-6//I10' becomes the integer array [71, 69, 4, 23]. These are stored in highly efficient tensor files.

[Point to Output Files]
The final artifacts are: train and test safetensors files, the vocabulary CSV mapping codes to IDs, the merged static data, and temporal statistics for time interval prediction.

[Emphasize Why Safetensors]
We use safetensors instead of older pickle format because it's faster to load, more secure against malicious code injection, and more memory efficient. When you're training large models, these details matter for performance."

SLIDE 9: MODEL TRAINING & ARCHITECTURE
=======================================
[Duration: 2 minutes]

"Now we get to the machine learning itself. Let me explain our model architecture and training process.

[Point to Left Column - Architecture]
We used a GPT-2 style decoder-only transformer. This is the same basic architecture that powers large language models like ChatGPT, but scaled for healthcare data. 

Our model has just 2 layers compared to GPT-2's 12 or GPT-3's 96. It has 128 embedding dimensions compared to GPT-3's 12,288. This gives us a tiny 0.41 million parameters.

[Acknowledge the small size]
Yes, this is extremely small by modern standards. GPT-3 has 175 billion parameters - our model is about 425,000 times smaller. But there's a good reason for this.

[Point to Design Rationale]
My laptop GPU has only 4GB of memory. A production-scale model with 50 million parameters wouldn't fit. By keeping the model small, we could train quickly, iterate rapidly, and validate the entire pipeline. Once we move to HyperGator's A100 GPUs with 40GB memory each, we'll scale up dramatically.

[Point to Right Column - Training]
We trained on 356 patients with 17,429 total tokens. Our batch size was just 4 sequences at a time due to GPU memory constraints, but we used gradient accumulation of 8, which effectively simulates a batch size of 32 for more stable learning.

We trained for 5,000 iterations, which took only 6 minutes on the GTX 1650.

[Point to Training Progress]
Look at the loss curve. We started at 4.28 - the model had no idea what it was doing. By iteration 500, loss dropped to 1.84. The best performance came at iteration 1400 with a loss of 0.63, so we saved that checkpoint. By iteration 5000, loss was 0.34.

[Explain what loss means]
Loss measures how wrong the model's predictions are. Lower is better. The steady decrease shows the model is genuinely learning patterns in the clinical data.

[Point to checkpoints]
We saved two checkpoints: the best model from iteration 1400, and the final model from iteration 5000. Each is only 5.7 megabytes - small enough to email.

[Bottom banner]
The key message: even with limited data and a small model, we successfully learned clinical patterns. This validates the approach."

ADDITIONAL Q&A PREPARATION
===========================

Anticipated Questions and Suggested Answers:

Q: "Why not use the full MIMIC-IV dataset from the start?"
A: "Great question. Full MEDS extraction takes 8-12 hours, tokenization another 18-24 hours, and training several days. If we hit an error at hour 20, we've lost 20 hours. With the sample dataset, we completed everything in under an hour, hit 20+ errors, fixed them all, and validated the entire pipeline. Now when we scale up, we know it'll work. Time to success: weeks with sample approach, possibly months with full dataset first."

Q: "How do you ensure patient privacy?"
A: "MIMIC-IV data is already de-identified according to HIPAA Safe Harbor standards. All dates are shifted, names removed, IDs anonymized. The data stays on HyperGator's secure servers - we only downloaded the processed MEDS files which contain no identifiable information. For UF Health data, we'll follow the same protocols plus additional IRB approval and data use agreements."

Q: "Can this predict other outcomes besides ICU mortality?"
A: "Absolutely. The architecture is task-agnostic. We initially tried hospital readmission prediction but lacked the necessary DRG codes in our sample. With full data, we can predict: 30-day readmission risk, length of stay, complications, need for specific interventions, even optimal treatment pathways. The model learns general clinical patterns, then we fine-tune for specific prediction tasks."

Q: "What about model interpretability - can doctors understand why it makes predictions?"
A: "This is crucial for clinical adoption. Transformer models have attention mechanisms that show which past events most influenced a prediction. We can generate explanations like 'high confidence of mortality because: previous respiratory failure diagnosis, age >75, three prior ICU stays.' The MEDS format helps because codes are human-readable medical concepts, not raw sensor data."

Q: "How does this compare to existing clinical risk scores?"
A: "Traditional scores like APACHE-II or SOFA use 10-20 hand-selected features and fixed formulas. Our approach learns from hundreds of features and complex temporal patterns automatically. Published studies show transformer models outperform traditional scores by 10-15 percentage points in AUROC. However, traditional scores are more interpretable and have decades of clinical validation, so in practice we'd likely use both - traditional scores for baseline and our model for enhanced accuracy."

Q: "What was the biggest lesson learned?"
A: "Defensive programming and configuration flexibility. Healthcare data is messy and varies wildly between institutions. Code that assumes full, clean data will fail in the real world. Every function needs to check: does this column exist? Is it empty? Is it the right format? And configurations need to adapt to what's actually available. The 20+ fixes we made aren't bugs - they're features that make the system robust to real-world conditions."
